How it works

Rolling, not daily.

Some Saturdays have forty fixtures. Some Tuesdays have four. Pick Hub follows the schedule instead of forcing a number.

1

The model watches the whole slate

Nine sports, every time zone. Each fixture gets scored against the market as it approaches — form, rest, injuries, confirmed lineups, venue, weather where it counts.

2

Picks post an hour out

One hour before each event, any fixture where the model disagrees with the market gets published — with the exact price on the board at that moment. Timestamped. No edits after.

3

Results post themselves

When it settles, the pick updates with the outcome and where the line closed. Losing picks are never removed. The board is the whole board.

Questions

Answered straight.

How many picks a day?

However many the schedule earns. A packed Saturday might produce a dozen across nine sports; a quiet Tuesday might produce two. There's no quota — if the model doesn't disagree with the market on a fixture, there's no pick, and that's the point.

Why an hour before, and not earlier?

Because an hour out, the lineups are confirmed and the injury news is in. Post earlier and you're guessing at team sheets. The tradeoff is honest: the market has also had time to price that news, so our edge over the closing line will look smaller than a service posting twelve hours out. We think a small real edge beats a large imaginary one.

Games run at 3am my time. Do I have to be awake?

No. Picks post an hour out and stay up. Turn on alerts for the sports you follow, ignore the rest, and the board is there whenever you open it.

Is this a betting app?

No. Pick Hub doesn't take bets, hold money, or connect to a sportsbook. It publishes forecasts. What you do with them is your call and your risk.

Will this make me money?

We don't know, and anyone who says they do is guessing or lying. Betting markets are efficient enough that most people lose over time — including most people using models. Read the record, judge it yourself, and never stake money you need.

What is "closing line value"?

The gap between the price when we posted and the price right before the event started. If the market keeps moving toward our call after we publish, the model is finding something real. If it doesn't, we're guessing with extra steps. It's the honest metric, so we lead with it.

What happens when the model goes cold?

You see it, in the same place and the same size as the wins. There's no separate page for the bad weeks.
